    • Design Narrative: Spoitube

    Introduction

    Okay. Let's say, in a hypothetical world, you were given the free will to design anything that you wanted. What would you choose? Would it be something personal to you? Would it be something you've always dreamed of having yourself? Or would it be the start of something you believe has the potential to be the next Snapchat, Uber, or Spotify?

    The beautiful thing about design is anyone can do it, but where do you start? And how you progress is where the real challenge lies, or for me, the fun.

    I started with an idea that I had as a personal project. What would this look like as a product on the market today? If it was something that I would use, then there is a market for this, and thus I have something that has the potential to add value to users.

    The idea was set and the design process began. The personal project that this design project was based on would take a Spotify playlist and convert it to a YouTube playlist. It would allow anyone with access to YouTube to listen to the same songs on the Spotify playlist on either YouTube or the YouTube Music app.

    I wanted this product because my student discount for Spotify was running out and as a YouTube connoisseur, I have a YouTube premium subscription that comes with YouTube music. I needed to condense. Who needs two music streaming services? So again, as a YouTube connoisseur, the Spotify subscription had to go.

    User Stories

    I CAN be a user of this product, but even more so, I NEED to understand the other perspective of alternate users. My story was one perspective of the problem domain and in design, a common phrase is the designer is not the user. This is the stage where I asked how other people would use this product and what other things outside of the main function would add more value to the user interacting with this product. This led me to 2 user stories.

    1. Emily joins a music club at school that shares new music. They use YouTube as their platform of choice. She uses the product to move her eclectic Spotify playlist to participate in the club's activities.
    2. Robert wants to have all his music in one place. He currently listens to most of his independent artists on YouTube to help give them more revenue from YouTube's partner program. And other artists on Spotify. He uses this product to have all his music in one place for a cohesive library.

    Developing the North Star

    The design now has a user base to build off of. Now let's give ourselves a design foundation or a North Star. This North Star is comprised of the values that we are going to try to portray in the design and do well for our target users.

    The values (in word form) that I chose as the North Star for this design were: Exploration and Simplicity. I want to intrigue the user to explore the other elements of the product freely, learn, and find value outside of the main function to create a community for the product. This is something that I wanted to encourage the users to do because that is an element of making a product the best it can be: the community that uses it. If I were to expand the North Star values to three words, the third would surely add community.

    From this I decided to make two other “branches” of discovery: Learn and Community. These will have equal importance to the main transfer function in the hierarchy. I do not want to impede on the freedom of exploration that is our north star. There will be places where a user can explore and learn about different things relevant to music: the link to why all users would be using this product.

    The essence of SpoiTube is to streamline the musical experience across platforms, making it a breeze for users like Emily and Robert to manage their playlists. For Emily, it removes the friction of sharing her unique music finds with her school club. No longer does she need to manually search and rebuild her playlist on YouTube: SpoiTube automates this with precision. Robert, on the other hand, finds harmony in his listening habits. His support for indie artists on YouTube and his enjoyment of mainstream Spotify hits are no longer at odds. SpoiTube unifies his library, reinforcing his contribution to artist revenue while simplifying his user experience. This isn’t just about convenience; it's about enhancing the auditory journey of every music lover, paving the way for a more connected and seamless music-sharing culture and community.

    I made the target device a web application, designed to be web first, but with the ability to be responsive in the future. I think about it as this product is something that you take time to engage with on the web, like Medium or Reddit (where many users interact on desktop), but also can be used on the go on any web interface. I have just implemented the desktop variation in what I am showing now, but it is still in fact, designed with the ability to hold our design language on any device in mind.

    Low-Fi Designs

    Now that we have the elements for understanding the users and how they interact with the problem domain, we can get to sketching!

    The preliminary sketches reflect our core values: Exploration, Simplicity, and Community. The interface is intuitively mapped out, allowing users like Emily and Robert to navigate seamlessly. Emily, with her eclectic taste, will find the 'Learn' and 'Community' sections invaluable for discovering new genres and participating in discussions, potentially bringing new sounds to her club. Robert's desire for a singular music repository is met with ease as the sketches outline a straightforward process for transferring playlists. The 'Transfer' button is prominent, signifying its importance and utility. Both users will appreciate the uncomplicated layout that encourages exploration without overwhelming them, fostering a community of shared musical interests.

    Feedback and Iteration

    The in-class peer review was an illuminating chapter in SpoiTube's story. The proximity of the login buttons was a point of contention, prompting a redesign to make more intuitive the linear nature of that stage of the user flow. The transfer process received acclaim for its streamlined approach, a testament to our commitment to error prevention. Yet, the color scheme failed to entice exploration, a vital spark that was intended but was missing from the visuals. Each critique was a stepping stone, guiding us to a design that stays true to our values while inviting users to embark on a journey of musical consolidation.

    Prototype

    Our prototype has undergone a good amount of critique, emerging with actionable insights. The button hierarchy needed restructuring, with a suggestion to elevate the 'Transfer' function, signifying it as the main thing that the site is for. The font sizes on the playlist transfer page were too big. The 'Manage' button's background color could benefit from a lighter touch, a nod to our pursuit of simplicity. Our efforts to make the linear nature of the login buttons have been noted, yet there was still room to refine these signifiers further. Introducing a subtitle on the top bar was an echoed sentiment, a compass for new users to grasp SpoiTube's purpose at first glance as well as other images, logos, and the developments of “clouds” to aid in communicating what you can do and what the site is for on the home page.

    User Testing

    For user testing I interviewed 6 people from ages 15-29 and ranging demograppics about SpoiTube. Each interview took, on average, 40 minutes and I gain infomation about them, their music interest, and how they interacted with SpoiTube. I walked them through 4 tasks: Transfer Songs from Spotify to YouTube, Interact with the Community, Explore the Learn Section, and Find the FAQ Page. After walking through tasks, I gave the opportunity for them to as any questions they had and for them to give any thoughts and preferences on the product. Each time this was the most interesting part because I learned a lot about how information from the pre-test questions were linked to a particular desire or perspective on a feature. Getting more users to test would only improve the quality if this product.

    After user tesing I committed to some changes:

    • Refine CTA Button Copy:

      Fix: Revise CTA buttons to be more action-oriented.
      Justification: Enhances user assurance and error prevention, increasing retention and satisfaction.

    • Redesign Labels and Instructions for Clarity:

      Fix: Use direct text labels and concise instructions.
      Justification: Improves user understanding and navigation efficiency.

    • Overhaul Rating System:

      Fix: Align with familiar models like numerical ratings or like/dislike systems.
      Justification: Reduces learning curve, enhances user engagement.

    • Redesign Homepage:

      Fix: Implement clear messaging and visual cues.
      Justification: Sets the stage for the service, reduces bounce rates, fosters curiosity.

    • Enhance Central Design Elements:

      Fix: Redesign central box and key elements to be visually appealing.
      Justification: Draws users in, supports intuitive navigation, crucial for positive user experience.

    Challenge of Design

    Navigating the color scheme proved to be a designer's dilemma, teetering between minimalism and vibrancy. The feedback was as varied as a palette, with no clear favorite emerging from the user sessions and other feedback. Ultimately, I anchored my decision in the feedback that resonated most frequently: colors that were subtle, unobtrusive, yet conducive to a modern aesthetic. The backdrop, initially a canvas of bland white, evolved into a textured landscape upon users' insights. It's a delicate balance, ensuring the space feels alive without veering into visual noise. This journey underscored a cardinal design truth—empathy overrides personal preference, and the user's voice is the guiding light.

    The top bar, a compass for navigation, was a challenge of restraint and foresight. Every element had to justify its presence, every icon a purpose. The decision-making was meticulous and involved tracing user journeys and aligning them with our North Star values. The button copy, initially not sufficient, evolved through the feedback to strike a balance between brevity and clarity, ensuring each word guided the user intuitively.

    The triad of the main pages—Transfer, Learn, Community—stands firm in its commitment to our values. It represents the freedom intrinsic to our design, a freedom we chose not to compromise.

    Reflection

    Reflecting on the SpoiTube project, it's been a profound journey from a personal need to a service that can touch the lives of many. The process was as much about understanding others as it was about translating my needs into a universal solution. With every sketch and prototype iteration, the values of Exploration, Simplicity, and Community became more ingrained in the design, guiding each decision towards a product that's not just functional, but also a joy to use. It's been a reminder that design is never static; it's a living process that evolves with its users. SpoiTube stands as a testament to the idea that a designer's work is never done; there's always room to grow, to adapt, and to anticipate the future needs of our ever-expanding community of music lovers.

    Design is a journey without a final destination. While SpoiTube now encapsulates a moment of completion, the reality is that it's merely a snapshot of an ongoing evolution. The future beckons with further user testing, each session a potential goldmine for insights. The quest for refinement is relentless. Building out of the current framework, the design will adapt, morph, and grow—responsive not just in screen size, but to the ever-changing landscape of user needs and technological advances. SpoiTube's design lifecycle mirrors life, cyclical and perpetual, with every iteration a rebirth of ideas and possibilities.
