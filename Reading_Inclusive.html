    Chapter 12 of "Don't Make Me Think"

    Sometimes when making websites or apps, designers and developers worry about adding features for people with disabilities because they think it will take more time and make their design less attractive to most users. They also worry about things called "tech debt," which is like a pile of chores that keep growing if they don't fix or update parts of their project. When they add new features, sometimes old parts don't work well with the new parts, adding to the tech debt pile. Making designs that are good for everyone, including people with disabilities, is very important but can feel like a big extra task. Sometimes, these good features don't get added because the team is worried about the growing pile of chores or they're rushing to finish the project. This shows why it's important to plan for making things accessible to everyone from the start, so it doesn't become a big worry later on.

    Chapter 4 of "Design of Everyday Things"

    In situations where you encounter a device you've never seen before but need to use it to accomplish a specific goal, a structured approach can help overcome the unfamiliarity and enable successful interaction with the device.

    1. Develop a Theoretical Model: Formulate a basic understanding or a theoretical model of how you think the device should operate based on your prior knowledge and experiences with similar devices.
    2. Inspect the Device: Inspect the device for visible signs, labels, or indicators that might guide you on how to use it. Look for common design elements like buttons, switches, or touchscreens that are used in similar devices you are familiar with.
    3. Match Actions to Visible Signs: Try to match the actions you think are needed to accomplish your goal with the visible signs you have identified on the device. For instance, if you need to turn on a device, look for a common signifier like a power button.
    4. Experiment and Iterate: If the device doesn’t respond as expected, don’t hesitate to try different actions or look for additional signifiers that might help. It’s a process of trial and error to understand how the device operates.
    5. Seek Guidance if Necessary: If available, seek guidance from manuals, online resources, or individuals familiar with the device. This can expedite the learning process and help you accomplish your goal quicker.

    This tutorial outlines a systematic approach to interacting with unfamiliar devices to accomplish specific goals, embodying the essence of exploring and understanding the affordances and signifiers presented by the device to make the interaction intuitive and goal-oriented.
