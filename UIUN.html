    • UIUN Summit Negotiation Reflection

    Introduction

    Imagine a summit where designers from around the globe converge, not just to share ideas but to forge a path towards a more accessible digital world. This was the essence of the UIUN Summit. It served as a melting pot of creativity and advocacy in the realm of UI accessibility. Our challenge? To rethink the user interface of the Sarpy County election commission site, specifically focusing on streamlining the process of registering for a mail-in ballot.

    Representing the Intellectual Disabilities Community

    As a representative of the intellectual disabilities community, I was tasked with addressing their unique UI/UX needs. This demographic experiences the world in a way that often requires a more intuitive and straightforward digital navigation. Their needs are specific yet universal: content with a Flesch Kincaid reading ease score of 90 or above for clear understanding, a simplified display to avoid overwhelming the user, a guided process with visual aids for multi-step tasks, and large, easily interactable elements for those with motor impairments. These requirements, while tailored for this community, have a universal appeal, simplifying digital interaction for all users.

    The Summit: Negotiating and Prioritizing

    The summit was full negotiations and collaborations. We were tasked with the task of prioritizing the collective requirements of all groups. It was a delicate balancing act â€“ advocating for our community's needs while ensuring the final design didn't exclude others. The most intriguing part was witnessing the defense of specific requirements that could easily have been broadened to encompass a wider range of needs.

    My proposal found natural allies in teams representing rural communities, PTSD, and anxiety sufferers. Our combined advocacy led to significant priority changes, highlighting the universality of our design principles.

    Insights and Reflections

    The highlight for me was observing the debate and eventual resolution between the rural and Gen Z representatives, particularly over issues like internet load time. This discussion underscored the importance of considering a diverse range of needs in UI design.

    My greatest takeaway from the summit is the concept that inclusive design is about not excluding anyone. It may seem counterintuitive, but focusing on not leaving anyone out, rather than on including specific groups, leads to more universally accessible designs. This experience was a real-world emulation of the dynamics in UI design teams, emphasizing the iterative and collaborative nature of the design process.

    Conclusion

    The UIUN Summit was more than just a design exercise; it was full of representations of the challenges and triumphs that happen in the field of UI/UX. It reinforced the idea that creating digital spaces that are accessible and user-friendly for everyone is not just a noble goal but an achievable one. The insights gained here are not just theoretical but practical tools that I intend to carry forward in my journey in design.
